EDUCATION

Stanford University (2020-2025)

M.S., Computer Science | B.S., Symbolic Systems | Minor, Political Science

Undergraduate Career

Coming into Stanford as an incoming freshman during the COVID pandemic, I was interested in working in personally interesting disciplines and capacities that allowed me to use the knowledge that I was able to learn from Stanford classes and my peers to do impactful and meaningful work. While I initially leaned towards focusing on Political Science, eventually the Symbolic Systems program, and its interdisciplinary approach to studying the relationship between human and computational intelligence, caught my attention. I loved the focus on understanding this central relationship in its entire scope, from learning about the psychological understanding of the human mind, to debating and writing about integral philosophy about central questions of human existence, to understanding linguistic frameworks of grasping human speech, and to coding and learning about key principles and techniques undergirding computational breakthroughs in matching and even surpassing human capabilities. Moreover, I was fascinated by how the program equips its students with the ability to think and reason about the connections between the human mind and computational frameworks and how they inform each other, a question that became increasingly consequential with the widespread adoption of AI and LLMs during my time in undergrad.

Graduate Career

Throughout my time in undergrad, I found myself increasingly drawn to projects and classes that explored a specific, nuanced relationship between humans and computers: understanding how human needs and behaviors can and should translate into technological decisions. This focus on need, particularly in the context of human-centered design, matched really well with my goal, from when I started at Stanford, to use the skills that I had learned in contexts that were broadly impactful and meaningful. This path led me to extend my Stanford career by one year to complete a Masters in Computer Science, specifically with a concentration in Human-Computer Interaction. As part of the process I enjoyed the opportunity to further my understanding of computer science principles through work in algorithm theory, systems, probability, and AI. Moreover, I loved learning about all aspects of the design process, from user research and storytelling to ideation and then to building and testing real prototypes. But even more than that, I loved being able to do all this while doing project work that allowed me to delve deeper into a variety of domains, including accessibility tech, game design, and behavior change. The focus on centering and responding to the needs of real users in these projects helped prepare me for the human-centered work that I aspire to do.
