DietScan

Winter 2025 | CS 377Q: Designing for Accessibility

In CS 377Q, we learned about using the design process to create products that fulfill the needs of people with disabilities. As part of this class, a team of us students created a product helping BLV users with dietary restrictions physically shop for groceries. For this process, first we conducted 30 interviews, surveys and user studies total to understand potential users’ needs and pain points. Next, we ideated through a cycle of continuous prototyping and testing using agile methodologies to create the final product. This final product used a phone camera to scan barcodes of a food product and return whether the user could or could not eat them given their food restrictions.

Skills Used: User Interviews, User Studies, Agile Methodologies, Prototyping, Accessibility Design

How Do Coterms Find Their Place?

Fall 2024 | DESIGN 231 : Graduate Design Research Techniques

Coterm students are students who matriculate from Stanford's undergraduate program straight into a masters program while they are still undergrads, allowing these students to work on both degrees at the same time and use undergraduate classes to fulfill masters requirements. It is well-known what the benefits are of the program, but what is not as well known is about what potential downsides could be. In this project, our group interviewed all types of grad students on campus to understand the needs, desires, and issues of coterms. In line with the class theme of "Checking Out", we looked to better understand where coterms feel seen and unseen and where they see themselves in comparison to newly-matriculated grad students and where they can see problems and potential with the coterm program for themselves and for the campus community.

Skills Used: Needfinding, Data Coding, POV Development, Qualitative Research Methods

Portfolio Optimization using ML-Generated Inputs for the Markowitz Model and a Reinforcement Learning Approach

Winter 2024 | CS 229: Machine Learning

Within the finance world, the Markowitz model is a common mean-variance model authored by Harry Markowitz as part of his Modern Portfolio Theory that suggests a set of weights to invest within a given portfolio to maximize returns given a certain level of expected risk. For our CS229 project, we decided to pursue two machine learning extensions of this theory:

1. One of the most important inputs into the Markowitz model is expected returns, and this is usually calculated using a simple average of previous returns for a stock. We decided to use ridge regression to calculate expected returns and explore whether that would improve the predictions of our model.

2. We decided to write a reinforcement learning model inputting past returns to predict weights for a given portfolio and compare it to the Markowitz model.

Skills Used: Machine Learning, Reinforcement Learning, Linear Regression, Python, Numpy
